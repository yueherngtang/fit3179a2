B40 Shrinks as Malaysians Rise

Ethnic Groups Slowly Shift Away from B40


As Malaysia's economy grows, the B40 income group — once comprising 40% of households — is shrinking. This shift reflects significant efforts across ethnic groups to improve living standards and break free from poverty, marking a positive trend towards a more equitable future.

The demographic composition of Malaysia is predominantly Malay, accounting for more than half of the population. They are followed by the Chinese, Indians, and other ethnic groups. Notably, more than one-fourth of the Malaysian population are Malays within the T20 income group, highlighting the economic disparities that exist within the country.

Unemployment Spike In 2020

Gradual Recovery Follows in Recent Years


Malaysia's unemployment rate surged in 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic but has steadily declined as the economy recovered. Despite the initial disruption, the labor market has shown resilience, with recent years marking a return toward pre-pandemic stability.

East Malaysia Faces Job Strain

Sabah & Labuan Lag While Others Rebound


High unemployment rates continues to plague Sabah & Labuan, driven by limited economic diversification & reliance on industries vulnerable to global market shifts.

The disparity between these states & more urbanized areas stems from slower infrastructure development & fewer economic opportunities. Addressing this requires support for emerging sectors to boost job creation and close the employment gap.

Expenditure-Income Ratio Inflates

Kedah Struggles With Savings & Low Income


Kedah's financial challenges are highlighted by its high expenditure to income ratio, the highest among Malaysian states, indicating that residents are spending a large portion of their income, likely due to necessities outweighing financial resources.

This is compounded by Kedah having the lowest median income, which correlates strongly with its high poverty rate — the second-highest in the country.

Additionally, Kedah is among the top five states with the highest unemployment rates, reflecting a struggling job market. These economic indicators together paint a picture of a region where low income, high necessary spending, and significant job scarcity intensify financial insecurity.

Rising Costs & Jobless Rates Reshape Household Spending:

Expenditure Shifts to Health as Costs Climb


In Malaysia, economic pressures over the last decade have prioritized essential household expenditures like food, housing and utilities, underlining their critical role in family budgets.

Recent years have seen a notable shift towards increased spending on health, driven by job market uncertainties and the pandemic's impact, highlighting health as an increasingly critical component of personal expenditure.

Conversely, spending on non-essentials such as apparel, leisure, and alcohol & tobacco has declined, indicating a consumer trend towards minimizing discretionary expenses in favor of necessities.
